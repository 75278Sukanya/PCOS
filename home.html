WHAT IS PCOS?

PCOS is a hormonal disorder affecting 1 in 10 women — and most don't even know they have it. In some cases, it may cause your body to produce higher than normal androgens found in males, which throws everything off balance. PCOD (Polycystic Ovary Disease) is often used interchangeably with PCOS, though it technically refers to a milder condition and not metabolic disorder.On Bloome, we use PCOS, since it's the term backed by global clinical research.

Symptoms:

  • Irregular or missed periods
  • Excess facial or body hair
  • Acne and oily skin
  • Thinning scalp hair
  • Overweight or underweight(lean PCOS)
  • Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound in some women
PCOS may run in families. PCOS is a chronic metabolic condition that can persist beyond the reproductive years. Women with PCOS may have higher risk for a variety of health problems including insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes or obesity.

Causes:

The exact cause is unknown, but several factors work together:
  • Genetics: Many genes increase susceptibility, but no single gene causes PCOS.
  • Insulin resistance: One of the major drivers. High insulin levels stimulate the ovaries to produce excess androgens (male hormones).
  • Hormonal imbalance: Increased testosterone and other androgens interfere with normal ovulation.
  • Chronic low-grade inflammation: Can worsen insulin resistance and hormonal imbalance.
  • Lifestyle factors:Diet, obesity, stress, and environmental chemicals may contribute.

Long-Term Health Risk:

  • Type 2 diabetes
  • High cholesterol
  • High blood pressure
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Endometrial cancer
  • Anxiety and depression
Not every woman with PCOS develops these complications, but regular monitoring is recommended.

The Domino Effect

PCOS doesn't just affect your body. It affects your entire life. What people don't talk about enough is what happens beyond the physical. It can affect confidence too.

When your body is tired, it can be hard to show up for work, school, or everyday tasks. Motivation vanishes. Careers can decline. Unkind comments from friends, family, or even strangers can hurt you, especially when you're already fighting low confidence.

The outcome is stress, anger, anxiety, and withdrawal from social situations. Here's the harsh reality: stress worsens PCOS. Increased stress leads to higher cortisol levels, causing more hormonal imbalances and worsening symptoms. It creates a chain reaction that feeds on itself.

Body affects mind. Mind affects body. And the cycle goes on.

That's why PCOS feels so hard to escape. It's not just one problem — it's many, all connected, making each other worse.

Understanding the chain is the first step to breaking it.

Research

Absent or irregular periods are almost a sign of hormonal imbalance. But, the cause isn't always the same. Understanding which hormones are involved is the first step to making the right lifestyle changes.

Multiple causes

PCOS is common, but thyroid issues, stress, and low body weight can cause the same symptoms.

Hormones are the key

Each cause disrupts a different hormone - identifying which one shapes the right approach.

Lifestyle matters

Diet, exercise, and sleep have measurable effects on hormonal balance, backed by research.

Common causes at a glance:

  1. PCOS

    Ovulation stalls due to excess androgens and insulin resistance.

  2. Thyroid disorder

    Both hypo and hyperthyroidism can disrupt the menstrual cycle.

  3. Chronic stress

    High stress suppresses estrogen, signalling the body to stop reproduction.

  4. Low body weight/underating

    Energy deficiency shuts downs reproductive hormones as a protective response.
